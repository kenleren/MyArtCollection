Collector workflow

Collector records that age well

A useful private art record does not need to solve every research question. It needs to make the next careful question easier.

Archivale Collector Notes · 5 minute read

Many private collections begin as memory. You know where a piece came from, which room it belongs in, what the seller said, and which envelope holds the receipt. A few years later, the object is still present, but the context has started to scatter.

Archivale is shaped around a simple idea: keep the record close to the artwork, and keep the record honest. The goal is not to turn a collector note into an appraisal, attribution, authentication, or insurance decision. The goal is to preserve the material you have so a future conversation starts from a clearer place.

Start with the object

Good records begin with what you can observe and confirm. Capture clear photos of the front, back, signature or mark, frame, labels, and any condition details you want to revisit. Add the current room or storage location in plain language.

Keep documents beside the story

Receipts, invoices, gallery notes, certificates, prior appraisals, shipping documents, and conservation notes are easiest to use when they live with the artwork record. A document can be valuable context without becoming a guarantee. Label where it came from and when you added it.

Separate facts from notes

A collector archive is stronger when it distinguishes confirmed details from memory, research leads, and questions. Use direct language such as "from purchase receipt," "family note," or "needs review" so future readers can understand the source of each detail.

Review gently, not constantly

A quarterly or seasonal pass is often enough for a private home collection. Check whether new purchases have photos, whether moved works have updated locations, and whether important documents are attached. The record should support collecting, not replace it.

Archivale's boundary

Archivale helps organize private collection information. It does not certify authenticity, determine market value, provide official appraisal conclusions, or approve insurance coverage.
