Acquisition records

What to Record After Buying Artwork

The best time to create an artwork record is while the purchase details are still easy to find. Capture the basics, attach the receipt, and label what still needs review.

Archivale Collector Notes · 6 minute read

After buying artwork, the paperwork and memory are briefly in one place. A receipt is still in your inbox. The seller description is still visible. The artwork may still be wrapped, framed, or waiting to be hung. That is the moment to make a practical private record.

The record does not need to settle every attribution, value, or provenance question. It should preserve what came with the purchase and make open questions visible.

Save the purchase source

Attach the receipt, invoice, gallery note, auction confirmation, seller email, or bill of sale if you have one. Record the seller or source name, purchase date, and any item number or lot number that appeared in the transaction.

If the seller description includes title, artist, date, medium, size, or condition language, keep that language connected to the source. Do not rewrite it as a stronger statement than the source supports.

Create the first photo set

Photograph the artwork soon after arrival. Capture the full front, reverse when safe, frame, labels, edition marks, signatures, packaging labels, and any visible condition details. If the work arrived with documents, photograph or attach those too.

Fresh photos help later conversations because they show what was visible near the acquisition date.

Record user-provided value carefully

The purchase price can be useful private context. Label it as a purchase price, not as current market value. If you add an insurance value or replacement estimate, label it as user-provided unless it comes from a specific professional document attached to the record.

Archivale records should keep value context clear without making appraisal, tax, legal, or coverage claims.

Track where it goes first

Add the first location: room, wall, storage area, studio, framer, or other custodian. If the artwork is waiting for framing, conservation review, photography, or storage, record that as the current status.

Location is often the first detail lost after acquisition. A simple note can save time later.

Use a first-record checklist

Item Record Careful wording
Source Seller, gallery, auction, or family source. "Listed by seller as..." when copied from source text.
Receipt Invoice, email, or transaction record. Supports purchase context; it is not a full history.
Photos Front, back, labels, marks, frame, and condition details. Visible on acquisition date.
Value context Purchase price or user-provided value. Not current market value unless separately reviewed.

Make the first record while context is fresh

A purchase record is strongest when it keeps source documents, photos, location, and open questions together from the beginning.
