How to Prepare Artwork Records Before a Move

Before an artwork is moved, loaned, reframed, stored, or handed to another custodian, a dated private record helps preserve context that is easy to lose in transit.

Archivale Collector Notes · 6 minute read

Artwork moves for many ordinary reasons: a home move, a rehang, a family handoff, framing, storage, photography, conservation review, a private loan, or a conversation with an adviser. The object may be handled carefully, but the context around it can still scatter.

A pre-move record gives the next person a clear starting point. It shows what the artwork looked like before the move, where it was, which documents were attached, and which details still needed review.

Confirm the artwork identity

Start by checking that the record describes the right object. Confirm the title or plain description, artist or maker if known, medium, dimensions, inventory identifier, and primary photo. If a detail is uncertain, keep that uncertainty visible.

This is especially useful when several works have similar titles, frames, editions, or subject matter. A private record should make it easy to match the object in hand with the record on screen.

Take fresh condition photos

Photograph the artwork before it leaves its current place. Include the full front, full back, frame, labels, marks, hanging hardware, packaging details if relevant, and any visible condition issue.

Date the photos and keep them with the artwork record. The goal is not to create a professional condition opinion. The goal is to preserve a clear observation of visible context before the move.

Write the movement context

Add a short movement note: where the artwork is now, where it is going, why it is moving, who is responsible for it, and when the move is expected to happen. If the work is going to a framer, storage provider, conservator, family member, adviser, or exhibition contact, record the role rather than sensitive details you do not need in the archive.

If the move is informal, the note can stay simple. "Moved from dining room to upstairs storage on 12 March" is more useful than no record at all.

Attach the documents that travel with the artwork

Some documents should be near the object before it moves: purchase receipt, prior condition note, framing invoice, shipping record, loan note, storage note, conservation correspondence, certificate, or family note. Attach copies to the private record when you have them.

Keep the boundary clear. Supporting records help explain context. They do not by themselves authenticate the artwork, appraise its value, settle ownership questions, or confirm insurance coverage.

Record what should be checked on return

A move record should help you inspect the artwork when it comes back or reaches its new place. Note details to review later: frame corners, glazing, backing, labels, hardware, surface marks, packing materials, or any existing condition detail that should not be mistaken for new damage.

If you already see an issue, describe it plainly and attach a photo. Later, you can compare the post-move observation with the pre-move record.

Use a simple pre-move checklist

The best pre-move record is short enough to complete before the practical work begins. Use the same checklist for rehanging, storage, framing, and family handoffs.

Step What to record Why it helps
Identity check Title or description, maker if known, medium, dimensions, and inventory ID. Confirms the record matches the object before handling begins.
Current photos Front, back, frame, labels, marks, hardware, and visible condition details. Creates a dated visual baseline for later review.
Location note Current location, destination, date, reason for movement, and custodian role. Keeps movement context with the artwork record.
Documents Relevant receipts, prior notes, loan notes, storage records, or correspondence summaries. Keeps supporting context from separating from the artwork.
Return checks Specific areas to compare after the move. Makes the post-move review practical and repeatable.

Keep private details private

Movement records can include sensitive information: home locations, storage providers, adviser names, family contacts, or transport timing. Keep only what is useful for stewardship and avoid adding unnecessary personal details to exported material.

Archivale is built for private collection records. That makes it a good place to keep the working context around movement without turning every note into a public-facing document.

Close the loop after the move

When the artwork reaches its new location, add a post-move note. Confirm the new location, add any new photos, record whether the planned checks were completed, and attach any new document that came from the move.

A simple before-and-after trail is often enough: pre-move record, movement note, and post-move observation. It gives the next review a dated sequence instead of a vague memory.

Make movement visible

A move record should show where the artwork was, where it went, what it looked like before handling, and what should be checked later. Keep the record factual, dated, and private by default.
