How to Record Artwork Labels and Inscriptions

Labels, inscriptions, stamps, and handwritten notes often carry the small details that make an artwork record easier to understand later. Record them carefully, with photos and plain source notes.

Archivale Collector Notes · 6 minute read

Many private artworks have useful information outside the main image: a gallery label on the back, a frame shop sticker, an inventory number, a handwritten note, an edition mark, a signature, or a stamp that someone once meant to explain. These details are easy to overlook until the artwork is reframed, moved, sold, inherited, or reviewed.

A good label and inscription record does not need to solve the artwork's history. It needs to preserve what is visible, where it appears, when it was recorded, and what remains uncertain.

Photograph before you transcribe

Start with clear photos. Capture the full front and back of the artwork, then photograph each label, stamp, signature, mark, edition number, frame label, hanging tag, and handwritten note separately. Include enough surrounding material to show where the detail sits on the object.

If text is small or faded, take one wider photo for location and one closer photo for reading. If glare, shadow, or glass makes the detail unclear, keep the unclear photo and add another attempt rather than replacing the record with a guess.

Describe where the mark appears

Location gives a future reviewer context. Note whether the label is on the reverse, frame, stretcher, backing board, mount, base, underside, margin, lower left front, lower right front, or another visible place.

For works on paper, be careful around mounts and glazing. For framed works, record what can be seen without taking the artwork apart. If a mark is partly hidden, say so plainly.

Transcribe plainly, then label uncertainty

Write down what the label or inscription appears to say. Keep the wording close to the source. Preserve unusual spelling, line breaks, numbers, dates, and punctuation when they may matter.

Use careful language for uncertainty: "appears to read," "possibly," "partly obscured," "not legible," "number visible but meaning unknown," or "needs review." This makes the record more useful than a confident sentence built from unclear evidence.

Keep source language separate from conclusions

A label can say useful things without proving them. A gallery label may list an artist, title, date, exhibition, or inventory number. A family note may identify where the artwork came from. A frame label may name a framer, place, or prior owner. Keep those source statements connected to the source that provided them.

Archivale records work best when they show the difference between "label says," "receipt says," "family note says," "collector confirmed," and "needs review." That separation protects the usefulness of the record without overstating what the source can support.

Record edition marks and numbers carefully

If the artwork has an edition number, plate mark, casting number, stamp, publisher mark, or printer mark, photograph it and write it exactly as seen. Include both the visible notation and its position on the artwork.

Avoid turning an edition mark into a valuation or authenticity statement. It is enough for the private record to say what is visible, where it appears, and which photo supports the note.

Use a compact label checklist

A short structure keeps the record readable. Use the same fields each time so labels and inscriptions can be compared later.

Field What to write Careful boundary
Detail type Label, signature, stamp, inscription, edition mark, frame label, or note. Name the visible detail, not what it might prove.
Location Front, reverse, frame, stretcher, backing, base, margin, or another visible place. Say when a mark is partly hidden or only visible through glazing.
Transcription The text, number, initials, date, or mark as seen. Use "appears to read" or "not legible" when uncertain.
Photo reference Which photo shows the detail and when it was taken. Keep the original photo close to the note.
Source note Whether the detail came from the object, a document, a family note, or a prior record. Separate the source from interpretation.

Keep the record useful for the next review

A label record is most useful when it can be checked later. Add the date, the artwork location, and any handling context. If a label is loose, damaged, covered by backing, or at risk of being lost during framing, record that as an observation.

If a future conservator, framer, appraiser, insurer, attorney, or specialist reviews the work, their professional notes should stay separate from the collector transcription. Archivale can keep both near the artwork record without blurring their roles.

Preserve the source, not the guess

A label or inscription record should keep the photo, location, transcription, and uncertainty visible. The value is in the careful context, not in turning a small mark into a larger claim.
