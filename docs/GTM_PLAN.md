# Go-To-Market Plan

## Recommendation

Launch as a paid, privacy-first collector records app for serious hobby collectors with 10 to 200 works.

Lead with:

- Fast cataloging
- Private records
- Google-backed storage ownership
- Insurance-ready reports
- AI-assisted intake

Do not lead with:

- AI art identification
- Appraisal
- Authenticity
- Investment tracking
- Museum-grade collection management

## Positioning

Category:

- Private art inventory
- Collector records
- Insurance-ready art documentation

Core message:

> Take a photo. Confirm the details. Keep every artwork record and document privately organized in your own Google account.

More direct launch wedge:

> Catalog your collection in minutes, keep the files in your own Google account, and generate insurance-ready records without hiring a registrar.

## Buyer Psychology

Collectors are buying:

- Peace of mind
- Control
- Proof of ownership
- Less shame about messy records
- A fast way to answer what they own, where it is, what documents exist, and what it is insured for

What converts:

- Private in your own Google account
- Photo in, draft record out
- Insurance/export/PDF report in minutes
- AI suggests, you confirm
- No fake authenticity or valuation claims

What weakens conversion:

- "Collection management platform"
- "AI appraiser"
- "Museum-grade software"
- "Investment alpha"

## Target Segment

Primary:

- US-first serious hobby collectors
- 10 to 200 artworks
- Collection value roughly USD 5k to USD 250k
- Contemporary, emerging, print, photography, works on paper, and small originals
- Self-managed records
- Willing to pay software prices, not registrar prices

Secondary:

- Family collection stewards
- Spouses or heirs documenting a home collection
- People preparing for insurance, moving, appraisal, or estate conversations

Avoid first:

- Museums
- Advisors
- Family offices
- Galleries as primary customer
- Ultra-high-net-worth private collection offices

## GTM Options Considered

### Option A: AI Art Identification

Pros:

- High curiosity
- Easy demo

Cons:

- Creates trust and legal risk
- Invites attribution and authenticity expectations
- May drive novelty use rather than paid retention

Decision: reject as primary GTM.

### Option B: Home Inventory For All Valuables

Pros:

- Larger market
- Insurance relevance

Cons:

- Loses art specificity
- Competes with generic inventory tools
- Harder to feel premium

Decision: reject for MVP.

### Option C: Privacy-First Art Records

Pros:

- Clear paid value
- Strong trust story
- Differentiated from spreadsheets and pro registrar tools
- Aligned with private Google-backed storage

Cons:

- Narrower market
- Requires strong execution and polish

Decision: recommended.

## Pricing And Entitlement Decision

Use a simple artwork-volume subscription with a separate usage pool for
variable-cost AI, online research, and document processing.

Reddit feedback from a former gallery director and a collections-management
adjacent commenter supports the private collector gap, especially for smaller
collections underserved by gallery-focused tools. The same signal also points
to real subscription sensitivity: the product should feel cheap enough to try
while still preserving paid value for growing collections and higher-cost
automation.

| Tier | Artwork volume | Manual records, photos, view/edit, export | AI, research, and document processing |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Free | Up to 5 active artworks | Included; users can view, edit, and export their own records | 1 AI credit/month |
| Starter | Up to 50 active artworks | Included, plus enough room for a small personal collection | 10 AI credits/month |
| Collector | Up to 200 active artworks | Included, plus room for a growing private collection | 50 AI credits/month |
| Archive | Unlimited active artworks | Included with no active-artwork cap | 200 AI credits/month |
| Optional credits/add-ons | Does not change manual cataloging access | Does not gate record access, manual edits, or export | Later credit packs for heavier AI, research, and document processing only |

Recommended starting price posture:

- Free: USD 0.
- Starter: USD 2.99/month.
- Collector: USD 4.99/month.
- Archive: USD 9.99/month.
- Revisit annual plans only after conversion, retention, and support-cost
  evidence supports the discount structure.

Safety and trust rules:

- Do not price around Google storage volume. Google owns that layer in the
  user's mind.
- Do not make export feel hostage-taking. Users must have a clear path to
  retrieve their data even after cancellation.
- Users who exceed the free artwork cap can still view, edit, and export
  existing records. The cap should limit adding more active artworks, not
  access to their catalog.
- AI credits must never gate manual cataloging, existing record access, record
  edits, or export safety.
- Optional credit packs are for variable-cost automation only, not basic app
  ownership.

## Validation Plan

Before deep implementation:

1. Create a landing page with three message variants:
   - insurance-ready records
   - private Google-backed storage
   - photo-to-record AI intake
2. Interview 15 target collectors.
3. Interview 10 partner prospects:
   - artists
   - small galleries
   - framers
   - appraisers
   - insurance-adjacent contacts
4. Fake-door test pricing:
   - USD 79/year
   - USD 99/year
   - USD 12/month
5. Concierge onboard the first 25 to 50 users.

## What Would Make This Wrong

Pivot away from this GTM if early evidence shows:

- Collectors care far more about valuation and market tracking than documentation.
- Strongest pull comes from galleries or advisors managing client collections.
- Hobby collectors refuse annual pricing above USD 60 to 80.
- Google-backed ownership is confusing or irrelevant.
- The true urgent use case is estate/family documentation rather than active collecting.

Next-best pivot:

- Advisor or gallery-assisted collection documentation.
