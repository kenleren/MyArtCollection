## Editorial Guardrails

- Write for private collectors and family collection stewards, not museums, advisors, galleries, or investors.
- Lead with practical recordkeeping: artwork details, photos, receipts, provenance notes, insurance values supplied by the user, locations, and exports.
- Use the trust model from `docs/COPY_TRUST_SPEC.md`: AI suggests; the user confirms.
- Do not claim authenticity, appraisal certainty, market value, attribution certainty, certification, investment performance, or insurance approval.
- Use "insurance-ready documentation" only to mean organized records that a collector can share, not an insurer-approved report, valuation, policy recommendation, or coverage guarantee.
- Mark market, insurance, legal, tax, competitor, pricing, and product-availability statements as needing citation and human verification before publication.
- Keep CTAs within current product truth: learn about Archivale, join a waitlist when available, or start organizing records. Do not promise unshipped workflows.
- Community and partner ideas remain observe-only unless a human separately approves participation under #92-style rules.

## Brief 1: Art Inventory Template for Private Collectors

- Title: Art Inventory Template for Private Collectors
- Target keyword / intent: `art inventory template`; informational search from collectors who want a simple structure before choosing software.
- Audience: Serious hobby collectors with roughly 10 to 200 works, family collection stewards, and spreadsheet users who need a cleaner first record.
- CTA: "Use the checklist to start your first private artwork record. Archivale is being built for photo-led records, supporting documents, and insurance-ready exports."
- Publish / readiness status: Ready for internal drafting. Human approval is required before publication.
- Can #124 safely use as a starter post now: Yes, as the first starter post, if it avoids screenshots that are not available and keeps product mentions framed as current positioning or planned workflow.

### Outline

1. H1: Art Inventory Template for Private Collectors
2. Intro: why a lightweight art inventory helps with memory, insurance conversations, moving, estate planning, and household organization.
3. The minimum artwork record:
   - artist or maker, if known
   - title or description
   - year or approximate date, if known
   - medium, dimensions, and edition details, if relevant
   - location
   - acquisition source and acquisition date
   - user-provided insurance value, if the collector tracks one
   - notes and tags
4. Supporting evidence to keep with the record:
   - front/back/detail photos
   - receipt or invoice
   - certificate, appraisal, condition note, or provenance note
   - report/export date
5. What not to make the template promise:
   - authenticity
   - current market value
   - appraisal-grade valuation
   - insurance approval
6. How Archivale maps to the template:
   - photo-led intake
   - human-confirmed fields
   - private records
   - attached supporting documents
   - insurance-ready export language
7. Short FAQ:
   - Can I start from a spreadsheet?
   - Should I include prices?
   - Are documents proof of authenticity?

### Proof / Screenshots Needed

- A simple field checklist table can ship without product screenshots.
- Optional later screenshot: blank artwork record form or populated sample record with fictional data.
- Optional later screenshot: export/report preview that clearly labels user-provided values.

### Trust Guardrails

- Say "user-provided insurance value" instead of "appraised value" unless an actual appraisal document is being described.
- Say receipts and certificates "support the record"; do not say they prove authenticity.
- Do not imply Archivale identifies the artist, authenticates the work, values the work, or creates an insurer-approved report.
- Keep all examples fictional or explicitly anonymized.

### Claims Needing Citation / Human Verification

- Any statement about what insurers require for art documentation.
- Any claim about how often collectors use spreadsheets or lose receipts.
- Any comparison to generic home-inventory templates or competitor products.
- Any mention of pricing, availability, or shipped import/export functionality.

## Brief 2: How to Document Artwork for Insurance Conversations

- Title: How to Document Artwork for Insurance Conversations
- Target keyword / intent: `document artwork for insurance`; informational search from collectors preparing for an insurer, broker, move, appraisal, or household review.
- Audience: Private collectors, spouses, heirs, and family stewards who need organized records but are not hiring a registrar.
- CTA: "Build one complete record per artwork, then keep receipts, photos, and notes together so the record is easier to review when needed."
- Publish / readiness status: Ready for internal drafting with strict insurance-claim review. Human approval is required before publication.
- Can #124 safely use as a starter post now: Yes, if all insurance statements are phrased as general organization guidance and citation-needed claims are left out until verified.

### Outline

1. H1: How to Document Artwork for Insurance Conversations
2. Intro: documentation is about making records easier to review, not guaranteeing coverage or valuation.
3. Create one record for each artwork:
   - confirmed identifying details
   - current location
   - photos and condition notes
   - acquisition details
   - user-provided insurance value, if tracked
4. Attach supporting documents:
   - receipt or invoice
   - appraisal document, if one exists
   - certificate or provenance note
   - repair, framing, or shipping records
5. Keep values separate from authenticity and attribution:
   - user-entered values are not app-certified appraisals
   - documents support the record but do not make a claim on their own
6. Export or share records carefully:
   - include report date
   - label user-confirmed fields
   - avoid implying official approval
7. When to ask a human professional:
   - coverage questions
   - appraisal needs
   - disputed attribution, ownership, or condition concerns

### Proof / Screenshots Needed

- Product screenshot showing attached document types, if available.
- Product screenshot or mock showing report/export date and user-provided value label.
- A fictional example record is acceptable until product screenshots are approved.

### Trust Guardrails

- Never say "insurance-approved", "coverage-ready", "guaranteed", "official", or "appraised by Archivale".
- Use "insurance-ready documentation" only with a definition in the post.
- Do not give legal, tax, valuation, or coverage advice.
- Clearly separate app organization from professional appraisal or insurer requirements.

### Claims Needing Citation / Human Verification

- Any insurer-specific documentation checklist.
- Any statement about required appraisals, scheduled personal property, claim handling, policy coverage, or legal duties.
- Any claim about how often art insurance records are requested or accepted.
- Any references to third-party insurer guidance, home inventory guidance, or professional appraisal standards.

## Brief 3: How to Organize Art Receipts and Provenance Notes

- Title: How to Organize Art Receipts and Provenance Notes
- Target keyword / intent: `organize art receipts and provenance`; informational search from collectors who have scattered invoices, emails, certificates, or gallery paperwork.
- Audience: Private collectors, family stewards, and spreadsheet users with documents spread across email, folders, boxes, and phones.
- CTA: "Create a record for the artwork first, then attach each receipt, certificate, appraisal, and note as supporting context."
- Publish / readiness status: Ready for internal drafting once screenshot/proof choices are settled. Human approval is required before publication.
- Can #124 safely use as a starter post now: Yes, if #124 uses no unavailable product screenshots or clearly marks them as placeholders for later replacement.

### Outline

1. H1: How to Organize Art Receipts and Provenance Notes
2. Intro: supporting records are most useful when they are connected to the artwork they describe.
3. Start with the artwork record:
   - title or description
   - artist or maker, if known
   - acquisition source and date
   - location
4. Sort documents by role:
   - receipt or invoice
   - certificate
   - appraisal
   - condition or framing note
   - provenance note
   - email or correspondence summary
5. Capture enough context:
   - document date
   - source
   - what it appears to support
   - whether the detail is user-confirmed or still uncertain
6. Avoid overstating what a document means:
   - a receipt supports acquisition history
   - a certificate may be relevant context
   - neither should be described by the app as automatic proof
7. Maintenance habit:
   - attach new documents when acquired
   - update locations after moves
   - export a dated archive before insurance, moving, or estate conversations

### Proof / Screenshots Needed

- Screenshot of document attachment flow or artwork detail document section, if available.
- Fictional example of a receipt attachment row with document type and date.
- Optional future proof: export/report showing attached document list.

### Trust Guardrails

- Do not call provenance "verified", "certified", or "proven" unless quoting a source with human approval.
- Do not say a certificate proves authenticity.
- Do not copy real private documents into public examples.
- Do not imply Archivale reads, validates, or guarantees document contents unless that workflow is shipped and reviewed.

### Claims Needing Citation / Human Verification

- Any definition of provenance beyond plain-language internal explanation.
- Any professional advice about what appraisers, insurers, estates, galleries, or auction houses require.
- Any examples using real institutions, artists, documents, or third-party standards.
- Any product claim about OCR, document extraction, or export behavior not yet shipped.
